Who We Are (Homepage Intro)
Section heading: A foundation born of one truth: Africa's ecosystems will not save themselves.

Body copy: Climate change is not a distant forecast for the communities we work with. It is the shrinking harvest in the Sahel. It is the river in the Niger Delta that runs black instead of clear. It is the heat building in classrooms, where children are trying to learn about a future their landscape may not support.

The Climate & Green World Foundation exists to confront that reality, practically, locally, and patiently. We plant trees. We restore degraded land. We make the case to farmers, communities, and government partners that bush burning, charcoal production, oil bunkering, and illegal artisanal refining are not survival strategies but slow-acting wounds. And we provide the alternatives that make a different choice possible.

We are new. Our founder spent years working as a lone voice on these issues before formalizing the foundation in 2025. The vision is bigger than the numbers. The numbers are growing every season.

Our Story
Hero headline: A foundation that started with one man and a question.

Body copy:

For years, Dr. Ike Anosike worked alone.

A scientist by training and a lover of nature by disposition, he watched the landscapes he had grown up loving, the forests, the riverbanks, the farmland of Nigeria and the wider Sahel — strain and shrink under the combined pressures of climate change, deforestation, oil pollution, and the slow erosion of community knowledge. He planted what he could. He spoke where he could. He documented what he saw.

In 2025, he made it official.

The Climate & Green World Foundation was registered with the Corporate Affairs Commission of Nigeria as a non-governmental organization with one purpose: to take the work he had been doing as an individual and turn it into something that could outlast any one person. A foundation. A team. A long horizon.

We started with what we had — a hectare of land, six hundred trees, ten people committed to the work. We are growing season by season. The vision is sustainable: a planet where nature thrives.

Overview
Hero headline: Tree-planting is the easy part. Everything around it is the work.

Body copy: Anyone can plant a tree. Making sure that tree is still alive in ten years, that the soil it sits in is healthier than it was, that the community around it benefits, and that the activities killing the surrounding ecosystem are being addressed in parallel, that is the work.

Climate & Green World Foundation runs four interlocking programs across Nigeria and the Sahel. Each one is designed to keep delivering benefit long after our direct involvement ends.


Our Approach
Section heading: Five principles that shape every project.
1. Community before planting.
We do not break ground on a project until the community that will live with it has co-designed it, agreed to it, and taken ownership of it. Top-down projects fail; we have seen it, and we will not repeat it.
2. The right tree in the right place.
Monoculture plantations are not forests. We plant native species in native configurations, guided by local ecology and traditional knowledge.
3. Restoration is bigger than reforestation.
Forests are only one kind of landscape. We also restore farmland, urban green spaces, and the ecosystems being eroded by petroleum pollution and unsafe land-use practices.
4. The conversation matters as much as the planting.
Bush burning, charcoal production, and oil bunkering are not abstractions; they are decisions made by real people under real economic pressure. Sensitization — done with respect, not lecture — is core to our work.
5. Measured in decades, not seasons.

How We Measure Impact
Section heading: What gets measured gets managed.

We track every project against a common set of indicators and report on them annually.

What we measure:

Tree and biomass growth — survival rates, canopy development, above-ground biomass
Carbon impact — tonnes of CO₂ equivalent drawn down or avoided, measured against baselines
Biodiversity return — species presence surveys, wildlife monitoring, habitat quality
Soil and water health — soil organic carbon, water table recovery, erosion reduction
Community outcomes — household incomes, sensitization reach, women's participation in leadership
Program integrity — cost per surviving tree, cost per hectare under management, funds delivered to the community

Our measurement framework will be built on the FAO methodology, IPCC guidance, and AFR100 reporting protocols, and will be reviewed annually.

Program 3: Petroleum Pollution & Ecological Defense
Tagline: The damage no one else is talking about.

What it is: This is the work that sets Climate & Green World Foundation apart from every other reforestation organization on the continent.

Across Nigeria, and especially in the Niger Delta, oil bunkering and illegal artisanal refining are quietly poisoning soils, rivers, mangroves, and air at a scale most environmental conversations refuse to confront. Add bush burning and unregulated charcoal production, and you have a set of practices that is undoing forest restoration faster than any planting program can keep up with.

We engage directly with this reality. Our work in this program is not extractive enforcement — we are not police. We are educators, partners, and advocates. We sit with farmers and community members. We explain what bush burning does to topsoil, what charcoal kilns do to forest cover, and what illegal refining does to water and lung health. We work with government and security stakeholders on the policy side. And we offer concrete alternatives wherever we can.

Key activities:

Community sensitization on the ecological consequences of oil bunkering and illegal artisanal refining
Engagement with farmers on the consequences of bush burning and charcoal production
Partnerships with government and regulatory stakeholders on enforcement and rehabilitation
Restoration work in petroleum-affected sites
Research and documentation of pollution's ecological footprint

Why this matters: 

Reforestation efforts that ignore petroleum pollution and forest-burning practices are incomplete. We cannot plant our way out of a problem that is being made worse upstream. This program addresses the upstream.

DEVELOPER & DESIGN NOTES
1. Color system. Pull both greens directly from the logo. The deep forest green for headers, navigation, and primary text. The bright lime green for CTAs, highlights, and the small graphic accents that bring warmth. Approximate values, to be confirmed from the logo: dark green #1C5E2C, lime #3ABE2C. Use white space generously — the brand should breathe.

2. Typography — important note. The Foundation has expressed a preference for EB Garamond, Comic Sans, or Comfortaa.

EB Garamond is excellent. Classic, warm, and corporate-credible. Strongly recommended as the primary serif — ideal for headlines, body text, and longer reading.
Comfortaa is acceptable as a friendly secondary face for accents, callouts, and small UI elements.
Comic Sans should not be used. It will damage the Foundation's credibility with the corporate sponsors and government partners that are the website's primary target audience. Comic Sans carries strong informal connotations that conflict with the "warm + corporate" voice we are aiming for.

Recommended pairing: EB Garamond for headlines and body. Comfortaa for accents only (e.g. button labels, small captions, infographic numbers). For UI text where neither feels right, fall back to a clean, readable sans like Inter or Source Sans Pro.

3. Imagery priority. This site will live or die on its photography. The Foundation has noted that no site photography is currently available. Two paths forward:

Short-term (launch): Use carefully chosen, royalty-free photography of Nigerian and Sahelian landscapes. Avoid generic "African child holding seedling" imagery. Lean toward landscape, soil, light-on-tree imagery rather than people, until original portraits are available.
Medium-term (within 6 months): Budget for a photo shoot at the Foundation's primary project site. Original photography of real CGWF work is a non-negotiable upgrade for credibility, and the difference will be visible immediately.

4. Honesty as a design principle. Resist the temptation to design as if the Foundation is bigger than it is. The Year-One framing on the homepage is a feature, not a limitation. Sponsors who choose to back a young foundation know what they are choosing — and they will trust honest growth numbers far more than padded ones.

5. Mobile first. In Nigeria and the Sahel, the overwhelming majority of visitors will arrive on mobile. Design mobile-first.
